Why did Creighton’s men’s basketball team fall off in 2026?

Creighton University has maintained a notably successful men’s college basketball program, with seven March Madness appearances in the past decade, including a run to the Elite Eight in 2023.

However, this past season, they did not qualify for the March Madness tournament and finished with a 15-17 overall record. What factors might have contributed to this decline in performance for the Bluejays?

First, let’s analyze their strength of schedule and simple rating system over the past two seasons to determine if the drop is due to internal issues, such as poor player performance, or external factors like an increased strength of schedule.

Sports Reference offers both a simple rating system and a strength of schedule metric for each team. The simple rating combines average point differential and strength of schedule, meaning teams could have many losses but still earn a positive rating if they faced strong opponents.

Next, let’s compare the rest of the Big East’s strength of schedule and simple ratings to put things in perspective.

However, that success did not last. In 2026, the Bluejays’ simple rating dropped by -6.14 points, while their strength of schedule decreased by only -0.78.

This indicates that from 2025 to 2026, while their schedule remained relatively consistent, their simple rating declined significantly, suggesting that the decline in performance is due to internal factors, such as team performance, rather than increased competition.

Having established that the issue is internal, let’s pinpoint whether it stems from offensive or defensive weaknesses and identify the root cause.

The average point distribution between this season and last is not notably different, so we cannot conclude whether this is primarily an offensive or defensive issue. Let’s consider turnovers instead.

Now we see something: Creighton is turning the ball over less, while their opponents are also committing fewer turnovers.

Next, let’s review last season’s roster to identify who had the most turnovers and whether they are still with the team, transferred or graduated.

Code 
c25 <- read_csv("creightonroster25.csv")
c26 <- read_csv("creightonroster26.csv")

creighton2526 <- bind_rows(
  c25,
  c26)

creighton2526long <- creighton2526 |> 
  pivot_longer(cols=starts_with("TOV"), 
    names_to="type", 
    values_to="turnovers") |> 
  mutate(type = factor(type, levels = c("TOV25", "TOV26")))

creighton2526long <- creighton2526long |>
  group_by(Player) |>
  mutate(total_tov = sum(turnovers, na.rm = TRUE))

ggplot() + 
  geom_bar(data=creighton2526long,
           aes(x=reorder(Player, total_tov), weight=turnovers, fill=type)) + 
  coord_flip() +
  scale_fill_manual(
    name = "Turnovers", 
    values = c("#6CADDE", "#005ca9"), 
    labels = c("2025 Turnovers", "2026 Turnovers")
    )+ 
  labs(
    x="Player", 
    y="Turnovers", 
    title="Creighton lost a lot of their rebounders this past \nseason.", 
    subtitle="Creighton had totaled around 440 turnovers in 2025, while in 2026, only having  383. That is 57 less \nturnovers this season compared to last season.",

Based on these observations, I believe that the loss of key players like Ryan Kalkbrenner and injuries such as Owen Freeman’s knee injury and Jackson McAndrew’s foot injury have significantly contributed to the team’s poorer performance this season.

With Head Coach Greg McDermott’s retirement, it will be interesting to see how Creighton progresses in the upcoming seasons. Is this the start of a decline, or can they recover from this disappointing season?
